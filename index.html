Child Labour

By Ayaan V, Tristan and Aayush

The Situation

Countries such as India, China, and a lot of countries in Africa, children are left to work all day. This happens without getting proper education for affecting their lives in future. In a country such as India where a lot of the population is in poverty, children are told by their parents that they should work hours a day just to be able to provide a living for their families. This also becomes a massive problem when children are unable to get a quality education where they can succeed in life but are often told they can’t because of the economic pressure they face. In places like China and countries in Africa, Millions of children are abducted and sent to cities where there is a low amount of adults to work so these kids are sold to employers and are stuck working long hours each day for such little pay. A big reason why these children are forced into child labor is that a lot of these families usually have not a lot of money to work with meaning their kids are forced into child labour.

What is child Labour?

Child labour is the employment of children in activities that deprive them of their childhood, potential, and dignity, often harming their physical and mental development. This includes hazardous work, child slavery, forced labour, and exploitative domestic work. The prevalence of child labour is driven by various factors, and it has significant adverse effects on the affected children and society.

Why is Child Labour caused?

Poverty is a primary driver of child labour, as families often rely on the additional income from their children to survive. Lack of access to education, cultural factors, economic demands for cheap labour, and weak legal frameworks further perpetuate this issue. These underlying causes create a complex environment where child labour thrives, especially in developing regions.

Consequences of child labour

The consequences of child labour are severe. Children working in hazardous conditions face serious health risks, including injuries and chronic illnesses. Missing out on education limits their future opportunities, trapping them in a cycle of poverty. Moreover, the psychological impact of exploitation can cause long-term mental health issues. Societally, the dependence on child labour hinders economic development by reducing the skilled workforce needed for future growth.

Efforts to stop Child Labour

International conventions like ILO Conventions No. 138 and 182 set standards for minimum employment age and the elimination of the worst forms of child labour. National laws, aligned with these standards, and global initiatives such as the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) aim to eradicate child labour. Organizations like UNICEF and movements like the Global March Against Child Labour play crucial roles in these efforts, focusing on education, economic support, legal enforcement, and awareness campaigns.

4. What social actions can individuals, groups, and institutions (governments/schools/businesses) take to raise awareness and take action to address this human rights issue?

To address child labour, individuals, groups, and institutions can take various social actions. Schools and community organizations can raise awareness by educating the public on the consequences of child labour and advocating for children's rights. Governments can implement and enforce strict labour laws, ensuring that children are not exploited and that they have access to education. Businesses can commit to ethical practices, avoiding child labour in their supply chains and supporting fair trade initiatives. International organizations and NGOs can provide support and resources to affected communities, helping to lift families out of poverty and reduce the reliance on child labour.

Laws, policies, and measures play a crucial role in eradicating child labour. Governments should enforce existing laws, such as minimum age requirements and safe working conditions, in alignment with international standards like the ILO conventions. Policies that promote access to quality education and provide economic support to families can reduce the financial pressures that lead to child labour. Strengthening social protection systems, such as healthcare and unemployment benefits, can also alleviate the conditions that perpetuate child labour. Additionally, international cooperation and partnerships can help monitor and combat child labour globally, ensuring consistent efforts across borders
